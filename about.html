ulitza street names

About

I wanted to know what the most popular street names in my country were. Who were all those men and women I saw everywhere on street signs, some of which quite popular, some I've never even heard of before. Eventually, out of curiosity, I've extended my search to my neighbouring countries and, finally, to all the countries in the world.

Method

I've analyzed all the street names from OpenStreetMap (osm) and kept only those named after a person (eponyms). For each country in turn I've sorted the names by their frequency and I've added wikipedia links to them.

The osm data is not consistent from country to country and there is no clear separation between names of streets, bridges, squares, public buildings or any other similar landmarks. As a result, all such landmarks are taken into consideration, as long as they are eponyms. So street names was a bit of a useful lie to get you this far.

I've sometimes also included the names of fictional characters, or a group of people if those are well-known or important from a historical point of view.

To avoid duplicate entries, there is only one name allowed per city. Affixes like street, boulevard, square and similar ones, are removed. If more than one name refers to the same person, they are considered equivalent and are not counted more than once.

Besides the per-country eponyms frequency, having all this data allows for an extra piece of information. Who are the persons appearing most often around the world? The front page presents just that. Every count besides every name is clickable and lists the name of countries where that name appears in.

Limitations

I'm mostly familiar with the European names and those were the easiest to find out and fill wikipedia links for. Even the US, Canada, Australia and the UK are sometimes hard to figure out since they contain only the family name and it's not always clear who it refers to.

Arabic is another challange but one that I was able to overcome here and there. A knowledgable eye is still needed here, as well.

China, Korea and Japan are, unfortunately, a complete mistery to me. I cannot even figure out what the affixes are, so that I can remove them. India is mixed-up and it's hard for an outsider to figure out the structure of the osm data. Help would greatly be appreciated in these cases, as well.

If your favorite country or hero is missing or I've attributed some links to the wrong persons, the error is mine, and it is an error or an omission and not a deliberate act. I'm more than happy to correct or add the missing data if proven beyond doubt that the information provided is correct. Check the github page and, more importantly, the eponyms folder where all the eponyms and all the magic happens.

Etymology

ulitza is a non-standard way of writing uliță without using any special characters (cedilla, in this case) as to keep the pronounciation. It is a Romanian word meaning a narrow road, used most frequently for streets in small villages borderd on both sides by houses, in contradiction to the city boulevards, for example.

It is derived from a proto-slavic word, ulita, and it has many variants in most of the Eastern-Europeean countries, like ulica (Bosnia, Bulgaria, Croatia, Poland, Slovenia), utca (Hungary), ulice (Czech Republic), у́лица(úlica) (Russia, Ukraine) or вуліца (vúlica) (Belarus).
